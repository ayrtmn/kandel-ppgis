## Part 1: Introduction and Methodology

---

### Slide 1: Title

**Opening:**
"Good morning/afternoon. Today I'll present a case study on how participatory GIS can help resolve one of the most contentious land-use conflicts in Germany — the tension between agriculture and renewable energy."

**Duration:** 30 seconds

---

### Slide 2: Introduction

**Opening hook:**
"Germany wants climate neutrality by 2045. That means solar capacity needs to grow from 65 gigawatts to 400 gigawatts by 2040. But here's the problem — where do you put all those solar panels?"

**Key points:**
- "Land is limited and needed for agriculture, environmental protection, and renewable energy infrastructure."
- "On one side, farmers say: protect agricultural land, food security matters."
- "On the other side, energy advocates say: we need ground-mounted PV, climate targets matter."
- "76% of Germans approve solar nationally — but when you propose a solar farm next door, local opposition appears."

**Closing question:**
"The question is: Are these objectives mutually exclusive — or can both coexist?"

**Transition:**
"To answer that, researchers turned to a method called PPGIS."

**Duration:** 1.5 minutes

---

### Slide 3: What Is the Main Problem?

**Opening:**
"So what's the core problem? Koelman (2025) identifies a national-local gap."

**Key points:**
- "At the national level: support for renewable energy."
- "At the local level: resistance."
- "Greßhake et al. (2025) explain why this happens: people are excluded from decisions, local impacts are not considered, and planning is often too top-down."
- "In the paper, they try to solve this by using PPGIS."

**Closing:**
"The question becomes: Are these objectives mutually exclusive — or can both coexist?"

**Transition:**
"Let me give you a brief history of how PPGIS emerged."

**Duration:** 1 minute

---

### Slide 4: Brief History of PPGIS

**Opening:**
"PPGIS has a rich history going back to the 1960s."

**Key points:**
- "1969: Arnstein publishes 'Ladder of Participation' — the foundation for all participation theory."
- "1990s: GIS and Society movement emerges — first serious critique of GIS as instrument of control."
- "1993-1996: PPGIS gets its name and identity at NCGIA."
- "2000s: Internet revolution enables online PPGIS — massive scale-up."
- "2004-2012: Validation — Kyttä creates softGIS, Brown proves ~6% spatial error."
- "2019-2025: Application — Kahila-Tani analyzes 203 surveys, Rösch applies to renewable energy."

**Closing:**
"The story is: from theory → critique → definition → scale-up → validation → application."

**Transition:**
"Now let me show you how PPGIS works in practice."

**Duration:** 1 minute

---

### Slide 5: Methods — PPGIS

**Opening:**
"PPGIS follows a 5-step process."

**Key points:**
- "First: Identify stakeholders — who should be at the table?"
- "Second: Participatory workshops — bring them together."
- "Third: Define inputs — what criteria matter?"
- "Fourth: GIS analysis — translate preferences into spatial data."
- "Fifth: Evaluate and discuss results — what does the map show?"

**Closing:**
"PPGIS = Local knowledge + Spatial data + Collaboration. The result is more transparent, accepted, and legitimate planning."

**Transition:**
"So how does this work in a real case study? Let's look at Kandel."

**Duration:** 1 minute

---

### Slide 6: What Does Kandel Look Like?

**Opening:**
"The municipality of Kandel is in Rhineland-Palatinate, Germany. It's a rural area with about 16,000 residents, covering 69 square kilometres."

**Key points:**
- "66% of the land is used for agriculture, 21% for forests, 10% for settlement."
- "Here's what's interesting — 60% of Kandel's energy already comes from renewables. That's well above the national average of 36%."
- "They have two existing ground-mounted PV plants, each about 2 hectares."
- "Their goal is to become an 'Energy Plus Municipality' by 2050."

**Transition:**
"Now let's play a game to experience what this conflict feels like."

**Duration:** 1 minute

## Part 3: Results and Discussion

### Slide 12: Workshop Results (1) — Sankey Diagram

**Opening:**
"So what did the stakeholders actually value? Let's look at the numbers from Rösch's study."

**Key points:**
- "27 participants from various stakeholder groups."
- "189 points allocated: 142 points (75%) to suitability, 47 points (25%) to restrictions."
- "Restrictions: 40% protecting high-quality arable land, 32% nature conservation, 28% landscape protection."
- "Suitability: 25% sealed areas, 17% marginal agricultural land, 15% conversion areas, 11% grid proximity, 8% motorways, 7% flat roofs."

**The surprise:**
"Agricultural land appeared in BOTH lists — as something to protect (arable, 40% of restriction points) AND as something suitable for PV (marginal land, 17% of suitability points). This shows stakeholders aren't anti-solar — they're pro-smart-solar."

**Visual:**

**Transition:**
"So stakeholders developed the criteria. Now let's see what the GIS model produced."

**Duration:** 2 minutes

### Slide 13: Workshop Results (2) — Scenarios

**Opening:**
"With the criteria from Workshop 1, researchers used ArcGIS to model spatial scenarios."

**Key points:**
- "First, GIS mapped each criterion as an exclusion zone."
- "Second, suitability was ranked using the Analytic Hierarchy Process — AHP — weighted by stakeholder preferences."
- "Third, areas were classified as particularly suitable, highly suitable, or moderately suitable."
- "Fourth, 40 combinations were generated — that's 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 criteria values."
- "Fifth, 10 were selected with the climate manager and mayor — forming a gradient from least to most restrictive."

**The scenarios:**
- "Scenario 1 — least restrictive — 472 hectares available, 6.8% of the municipality."
- "Scenario 5 — moderate — 156 hectares, 2.3%."
- "Scenario 10 — most restrictive — only 29 hectares, 0.4%."

**The key finding:**
"Even under Scenario 10 — with ALL restrictions active: no arable land, no nature areas, 200-metre buffers, 10-hectare minimum — 29 hectares is still available for ground-mounted PV."

**Transition:**
"This finding changed the conversation. Now let's see what the authors concluded."

**Duration:** 2 minutes

### Slide 14: Paper Conclusion

**Opening:**
"So what did Rösch and Fakharizadehshirazi conclude? Three things."

**Aspect 1: Procedural and Distributive Justice**
"First: Procedural and distributive justice. The process was fair — stakeholders co-developed criteria together. The outcomes were fair — 29 hectares available for all. PPGIS doesn't just produce a map — it produces a fair process that leads to a fair outcome."

**Aspect 2: Fairness and Improved Mutual Understanding**
"Second: Fairness and improved mutual understanding. Farmers and energy advocates understood each other's perspectives for the first time. A participant said: 'I was positively surprised by what came out of the analysis.' PPGIS transforms 'my position vs. your position' into 'what do we collectively value?'"

**Aspect 3: Willingness to Compromise**
"Third: Willingness to compromise. The criteria were adopted by clear majority. Farmers who attended acknowledged the need for ground-mounted PV. Even under Scenario 10 — with ALL restrictions active — 29 hectares is still available. PPGIS produces outcomes that are 'good enough' for everyone — not perfect for anyone, but acceptable to all."

**The nuance:**
"But here's an important caveat from the authors: Participation increases legitimacy — but it doesn't automatically increase acceptance. Acceptance also depends on the specific process, context, facility design, and the willingness of the landowner to change land use."

**Transition:**
"The paper covers justice, understanding, and compromise. But there are three things the paper DOESN'T cover — and that's where our analysis begins."

**Duration:** 1.5 minutes

### Slide 16: Finding (1) — Conflict Resolution Mechanism

**Opening:**
"PPGIS is not just a mapping tool; it synthesizes all of the conflicts into structured and evidence-based deliberation and could become a conflict resolution mechanism in the future."

**Concrete Example:**
"In Kandel, 27 participants — farmers, nature protection, energy cooperatives, administrators — sat together for the first time. They each allocated 7 points across restriction and suitability criteria. Result: 189 points allocated, criteria adopted by clear majority."

**Illustration:**
```
Before PPGIS:
Farmers: "No solar on our land!"
Energy advocates: "We need solar everywhere!"
→ Deadlock

After PPGIS:
All stakeholders: "Let's map where solar makes sense together"
→ 29 ha available, criteria adopted by majority
```

**The evidence:**
- "A participant said: 'I was positively surprised by what came out of the analysis.'"
- "Farmers who attended acknowledged the need for ground-mounted PV."

**Connecting to the literature:**
- "Reed (2008) identifies eight best practices for stakeholder participation: empowerment, equity, trust, and learning. Rösch's workshop design follows these."
- "Strickland-Munro et al. (2016) applied PPGIS in marine spatial planning in Australia — found the same process benefits. The process works across contexts."

**Closing:**
"PPGIS is not just a mapping tool — it's a conflict resolution mechanism."

**Transition:**
"But PPGIS has limits. Let's look at the second finding."

**Duration:** 1.5 minutes

---

### Slide 17: Finding (2) — Partial Truth & Other Risks

**Opening:**
"PPGIS includes spatial analysis, but it is mostly based on social values — stakeholder preferences. This creates a partial truth that feels comprehensive."

**Concrete Example:**
"The 29 hectare finding: PPGIS says 29 hectares are available. But is that ecologically sound? Economically viable? Technically possible? PPGIS answers 'where people want solar' — but not 'where solar makes sense'."

**What PPGIS answers:**
- "People want solar there (preferences)"
- "Defined restriction zone (no high-quality arable land)"
- "Meets minimum distance requirements"

**What PPGIS doesn't answer:**
- "Is it a critical habitat corridor? (Ecology)"
- "Is it cost-effective given grid distance? (Economic)"
- "Is the area a water catchment area? (Ecosystem Services)"

**Other risks:**
- "Time-consuming, costly, may increase dissatisfaction (English et al. 1993)"
- "~6% spatial error, participation accuracy as a trade-off (Brown, 2012)"
- "'Illusion of control', data quality challenge (Sieber, 2006)"
- "Need comparative studies across contexts (Brown & Kyttä, 2014)"

**Closing:**
"PPGIS without other toolkits = partial truth that feels complete. With other toolkits = evidence-based decision."

**Transition:**
"So if PPGIS risks partial truth, what's the solution? Let's look at the third finding."

**Duration:** 1.5 minutes

---

### Slide 18: Finding (3) — Integration with Other Toolkits

**Opening:**
"The question is not 'PPGIS or expert assessment?' — it's 'how do we integrate both?'"

**Key points:**
- "For Kandel, a complete assessment includes 9 toolkits — each from a different topic paper in our course."
- "First: PPGIS — Topic 5: Rösch et al. (2025) — 'What do people value?'"
- "Second: Ecosystem Services Mapping & Assessment — Topic 1: Sieber et al. (2021) — 'What ecological benefits does this landscape provide?'"
- "Third: Landscape Aesthetic Quality — Topic 9: Hermes et al. (2018) — 'What is the visual/scenic value?'"
- "Fourth: Protected Area Quality — Topic 7: Cole & Johnson (2025) — 'Which conservation areas are truly effective?'"
- "Fifth: Landscape Connectivity — Topic 10: Lichtenberg et al. (2025) — 'How do species move across the landscape?'"
- "Sixth: Economic Valuation — Topic 8 — 'Is it cost-effective?'"
- "Seventh: Participatory Scenario Planning — Topic 6: Jiren et al. (2020) — 'What if we combine participation with future scenarios?'"
- "Eighth: Stakeholder Analysis — 'Who are the stakeholders and what do they want?'"
- "Ninth: Conservation Conflict Hierarchical Matrix — Silveira-Junior et al. (2021) — 'How do we diagnose and navigate conflicts?'"

**The Cascade Model:**
"These tools are connected by the Cascade Model: Biophysical structures → Functions → Services → Benefits → Values."

**Closing:**
"PPGIS provides the social foundation — what people value. Other toolkits provide ecological, economic, legal, and scenario foundations. Together, they create evidence-based decisions that are both legitimate and feasible."

**Transition:**
"Let me summarise the key takeaways."

### Slide 19: Takeaways

**Opening:**
"Let me bring everything together with three key takeaways from our analysis."

---

**Takeaway 1: The Conflict Is Not Mutually Exclusive**

"The agriculture-energy conflict is not a zero-sum game. Even under the strictest constraints — no arable land, no nature areas, 200-metre buffers, 10-hectare minimum — 29 hectares are still available for ground-mounted PV in Kandel. That's 0.4% of the municipality. Not a lot, but enough to make progress."

**Why this matters:**
- "This finding surprised even the workshop participants."
- "It shows that when you systematically analyse the landscape, there IS room for both agriculture and energy."
- "The question shifts from 'where do we put solar?' to 'how do we design a process that works?'"

---

**Takeaway 2: PPGIS Transforms Emotional Conflicts Into Structured Deliberation**

"PPGIS is not just a mapping tool. It's a conflict resolution mechanism. When 27 stakeholders — farmers, nature protection, energy cooperatives, administrators, and the energy supplier — sat together for the first time, they allocated 189 points across restriction and suitability criteria. The result: criteria adopted by clear majority."

**Why this matters:**
- "Before PPGIS: 'No solar on our land!' vs. 'We need solar everywhere!' → Deadlock."
- "After PPGIS: 'Let's map where solar makes sense together' → 29 ha available, criteria adopted."
- "Reed (2008) identifies eight best practices for stakeholder participation. Rösch's workshop design follows these."
- "Strickland-Munro et al. (2016) found the same process benefits in marine spatial planning in Australia. The process works across contexts."

---

**Takeaway 3: The Implementation Gap Is Closable — Integration Is Key**

"The implementation gap is not inevitable. Rösch handed the PPGIS tool to the municipality for future GM-PV project evaluation. But PPGIS alone is not enough. It needs to be integrated with other toolkits: ecosystem services mapping, landscape connectivity, protected area quality, economic valuation, and participatory scenario planning."

**Why this matters:**
- "PPGIS provides the social foundation — what people value."
- "Other toolkits provide ecological, economic, legal, and scenario foundations."
- "Together, they create evidence-based decisions that are both legitimate and feasible."
- "As Ofoegbu et al. (2023) showed: no single tool can address all dimensions."

---

**Closing:**
"So the question is not 'whose side are you on?' but 'how do we design processes that work?' The answer: integrate PPGIS with other toolkits, deliver tools to decision-makers, and let affected stakeholders develop the criteria together."

**Transition:**
"Now let's open the floor for debate."

**Duration:** 1.5 minutes

## Tips for Delivery

1. **Pace:** Speak slowly and clearly. Pause after key findings (especially "29 hectares").
2. **Eye contact:** Look at the audience, not the slides.
3. **Engagement:** Ask rhetorical questions ("Are these objectives mutually exclusive?").
4. **Visuals:** Point to the Sankey diagram when discussing Workshop 1 results.
5. **Quotes:** Read the participant quote with emotion — it's the human story.
6. **Game:** During the Kandel Game, walk around and observe stakeholder interactions.
7. **Fishbowl:** Let the Fishbowl Discussion run naturally — don't interrupt.
8. **Debate:** Let the audience debate — this is the engagement climax.
9. **Closing:** End with the takeaways, not the references. Leave the audience with the message.
