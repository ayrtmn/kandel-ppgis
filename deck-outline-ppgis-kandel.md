### Slide 3: What Is the Main Problem?

**National-local gap (Koelman 2025):**
- At the national level: support
- At the local level: resistance

**This happens when (Greßhake et al. 2025):**
- People are excluded from decisions
- Local impacts are not considered
- Planning is often too top-down

**In the paper, they try to solve this by using PPGIS**

**The question:** Are these objectives mutually exclusive — or can both coexist?

*Source: Koelman (2025), Greßhake et al. (2025)*

### Slide 4: Brief History of PPGIS

| Era | Year | Event | Significance |
|-----|------|-------|--------------|
| **1960s** | 1969 | Arnstein's "Ladder of Participation" | Foundation for participation theory |
| **1990s** | 1990s | GIS and Society movement | Critiques GIS as instrument of control |
| **1990s** | 1993-1996 | NCGIA defines PPGIS | "Making GIS accessible to all" (Schroeder 1996) |
| **2000s** | 2000s | Internet revolution | Scale-up from workshop-based to web-based |
| **2000s** | 2004-2012 | Validation (Kyttä, Brown) | softGIS methodology, ~6% spatial error |
| **2020s** | 2019-2025 | Application (Kahila-Tani, Rösch) | Largest empirical studies, renewable energy |

**Website created during the scale-up:** PPGIS.net (2015)

### Slide 5: Methods — PPGIS

**5-Step PPGIS Process:**

```
1. Identify Stakeholders
       ↓
2. Participatory Workshops
       ↓
3. Define Inputs
       ↓
4. GIS Analysis
       ↓
5. Evaluate and Discuss Results
```

**PPGIS = Local knowledge + Spatial data + Collaboration**

**Result:** More transparent, accepted, and legitimate planning

*Source: Sieber (2006), Brown (2012)*

### Slide 13: Workshop Results (2) — Scenarios

**How scenarios were created:**

1. GIS mapped each criterion as an exclusion zone
2. Suitability ranked using Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) — weighted by stakeholder preferences
3. Areas classified as: particularly suitable / highly suitable / moderately suitable
4. **40 combinations** generated (2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 criteria values)
5. **10 selected** with climate manager and mayor — forming a gradient from least to most restrictive

**10 Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Particularly Suitable (ha) | Highly Suitable (ha) | Moderately Suitable (ha) | Total (ha) | % of Municipality |
|----------|---------------------------|---------------------|-------------------------|------------|-------------------|
| 1 (least restrictive) | 162 | 188 | 122 | 472 | 6.8% |
| 2 | 143 | 150 | 98 | 391 | 5.7% |
| 3 | 116 | 115 | 86 | 317 | 4.6% |
| 4 | 56 | 88 | 34 | 178 | 2.6% |
| 5 | 56 | 66 | 34 | 156 | 2.3% |
| 6 | 44 | 49 | 33 | 126 | 1.8% |
| 7 | 23 | 42 | 0 | 64 | 0.9% |
| 8 | 23 | 29 | 0 | 52 | 0.8% |
| 9 | 23 | 18 | 0 | 41 | 0.6% |
| **10 (most restrictive)** | **11** | **18** | **0** | **29** | **0.4%** |

**Even under the strictest scenario — no arable land, no nature areas, 200m buffers, 10ha minimum — 29 hectares is still available for ground-mounted PV.**

*Source: Rösch et al. (2025)*

### Slide 14: Paper Conclusion

**"I was positively surprised by what came out of the analysis, that if you apply all these criteria, there are enough areas where it would make sense to have such solar parks."**
— Workshop Participant

**PPGIS scenario approach supports 3 key outcomes:**

---

**Aspect 1: Procedural and Distributive Justice**

| Dimension | What It Means | Evidence from Kandel |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Procedural Justice** | The process was fair | 27 participants co-developed criteria together; transparent methodology; equal voice (7 points each) |
| **Distributive Justice** | The outcomes were fair | 29 hectares available for ALL stakeholders; balanced interests; no group excluded |

**Key insight:** PPGIS doesn't just produce a map — it produces a FAIR PROCESS that leads to a FAIR OUTCOME.

---

**Aspect 2: Fairness and Improved Mutual Understanding**

| Before PPGIS | After PPGIS |
|--------------|-------------|
| Farmers: "Solar is bad" | Farmers: "I understand why solar is needed" |
| Energy advocates: "Farmers are blocking progress" | Energy advocates: "I understand farmers' concerns" |
| Each group saw the other as enemy | Each group saw the other as partner |

**Evidence:**
- "I was positively surprised by what came out of the analysis" — Workshop participant
- Farmers who attended acknowledged the need for ground-mounted PV
- The 75/25 split showed stakeholders were more interested in WHERE solar should go (75%) than WHERE it shouldn't (25%)

**Key insight:** PPGIS transforms "my position vs. your position" into "what do we collectively value?"

---

**Aspect 3: Willingness to Compromise**

| Compromise | Evidence from Kandel |
|------------|---------------------|
| Criteria adopted by clear majority | Not unanimous, but strong support |
| Farmers accepted GM-PV | Farmers who attended acknowledged the need |
| Environmental groups accepted some PV | Not all areas protected, but key areas protected |
| Energy groups accepted restrictions | Not unlimited PV, but 29 ha still available |

**The key finding:** Even under Scenario 10 (most restrictive), 29 hectares still available. This means: Even with ALL restrictions active, there's still room for solar. The compromise: Protect what matters MOST, allow solar where it makes sense.

**Key insight:** PPGIS produces outcomes that are "good enough" for everyone — not perfect for anyone, but acceptable to all.

---

**Important caveat:** Participation increases legitimacy — but not automatically acceptance. Acceptance depends on process, context, facility design, and landowner willingness.

*Rösch and Fakharizadehshirazi (2025)*

### Slide 16: Finding (1) — Conflict Resolution Mechanism

**PPGIS is not just a mapping tool; it synthesizes all of the conflicts into structured and evidence-based deliberation and could become a conflict resolution mechanism in the future.**

**Concrete Example — Kandel Workshop 1:**
- 27 participants: farmers, nature protection, energy cooperatives, administrators
- They sat together for the first time to discuss where solar should go
- Each participant allocated 7 points across restriction and suitability criteria
- Result: 189 points allocated, criteria adopted by clear majority

**Illustration:**

```
Before PPGIS:
Farmers: "No solar on our land!"
Energy advocates: "We need solar everywhere!"
→ Deadlock

After PPGIS:
All stakeholders: "Let's map where solar makes sense together"
→ 29 ha available, criteria adopted by majority
```

**The evidence:**
- "I was positively surprised by what came out of the analysis" — Workshop participant
- Farmers who attended acknowledged the need for ground-mounted PV

**Connecting to the literature:**
- Reed (2008) identifies eight best practices for stakeholder participation: empowerment, equity, trust, and learning. Rösch's workshop design follows these.
- Strickland-Munro et al. (2016) applied PPGIS in marine spatial planning in Australia — found the same process benefits. The process works across contexts.

**The implication:** PPGIS is not just a mapping tool — it's a conflict resolution mechanism.

*Source: Rösch et al. (2025), Reed (2008), Strickland-Munro et al. (2016)*

---

### Slide 17: Finding (2) — Partial Truth & Other Risks

**PPGIS includes spatial analysis, but it is mostly based on social values (stakeholder preferences). This creates a partial truth that feels comprehensive.**

**What PPGIS answers:**
- People want solar there (preferences)
- Defined restriction zone (no high-quality arable land)
- Meets minimum distance requirements

**What PPGIS doesn't answer:**
- Is it a critical habitat corridor? (Ecology)
- Is it cost-effective given grid distance? (Economic)
- Is the area a water catchment area? (Ecosystem Services)

**Other risks:**
- Time-consuming, costly, may increase dissatisfaction (English et al. 1993)
- ~6% spatial error, participation accuracy as a trade-off (Brown, 2012)
- "Illusion of control", data quality challenge (Sieber, 2006)
- Need comparative studies across contexts (Brown & Kyttä, 2014)

**The pseudo truth risk:** The spatial analysis makes PPGIS LOOK comprehensive — but the analysis is only based on social values, not ecological/economic/technical data. Decision-makers may trust the output because it's "scientific" (GIS-based) — but it's incomplete.

**The implication:** PPGIS without other toolkits = partial truth that feels complete. With other toolkits = evidence-based decision.

*Source: Rösch et al. (2025), English et al. (1993), Brown (2012), Sieber (2006), Brown & Kyttä (2014)*

**The question is not "PPGIS or expert assessment?" — it's "how do we integrate both?"**

**Complete Assessment Toolkit for Kandel:**

**The Cascade Model connects them all:**

```
Biophysical structures → Functions → Services → Benefits → Values
     (soil, water)      (capacity)   (provision)  (reduced flood) (monetary)
```

**Connecting to the literature:**
- Ofoegbu et al. (2023) developed a holistic landscape decision framework — PPGIS is one component, not the whole solution.
- Mustalahti et al. (2026) found that integration across tools produces better outcomes than any single method.

**The implication:**
- PPGIS provides the social foundation — what people value
- Other toolkits provide ecological, economic, legal, and scenario foundations
- Together, they create evidence-based decisions that are both legitimate and feasible

*Source: Rösch et al. (2025), Ofoegbu et al. (2023), Mustalahti et al. (2026), Topic Papers 1-10*

### Slide 19: Takeaways

**What we learned:**
- The agriculture-energy conflict is not mutually exclusive — 29 ha available under maximum constraints
- PPGIS transforms emotional conflicts into structured deliberation
- The implementation gap is closable — deliver tools to decision-makers

**What this changes:**
- Participatory processes can produce policy, not just academic papers
- When stakeholders develop criteria together, they find common ground
- PPGIS is one essential tool among many — integration is key

**The bigger picture:**
- Landscape planning requires multiple perspectives: social, ecological, economic, technical, legal
- So we need comprehensive thinking to design a process that can produce ideal decisions
- The question is not "whose side are you on?" but "how do we design processes that work?"

## Tips for Delivery

1. **Pace:** Speak slowly and clearly. Pause after key findings (especially "29 hectares").
2. **Eye contact:** Look at the audience, not the slides.
3. **Engagement:** Ask rhetorical questions ("Are these objectives mutually exclusive?").
4. **Visuals:** Point to the Sankey diagram when discussing Workshop 1 results.
5. **Quotes:** Read the participant quote with emotion — it's the human story.
6. **Game:** During the Kandel Game, walk around and observe stakeholder interactions.
7. **Debate:** Let the audience debate — this is the engagement climax.
8. **Closing:** End with the takeaways, not the references. Leave the audience with the message.
